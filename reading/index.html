reading list

biology

The Ideas of Biology, Bonner, (1962)
This is a well-written discussion of some of the big ideas in biology. Bonner outlines a rapidly-changing field at a stage in its development where big contemporary trends were still beyond the horizon, but scientists were beginning to suspect their existence. An interesting illustration of biological principles, and also of the way that science takes shape.

The Double Helix: A Personal Account of the Discovery of the Structure of DNA, Watson (1968)
Like the title says, a first-hand description of how it all went down. Readable and interesting both for the science and for the view of science in a particular time and place. Notable insights include the importance of playing with a (pre-computer) 3d model to discover the structure of the DNA molecule, as well as the fact that the science basically took place in between going to the pub and trying to get invited to dinner parties with attractive European girls.

Molecular Biology for Computer Scientists, Hunter (1993)

Introduction to Computational Genomics: a Case Studies Approach, Cristianini & Hahn (2006)

An Introduction to Systems Biology: Design Principles of Biological Circuits, Alon (2006)

mathematics

How to Solve It: A New Aspect of Mathematical Method, Polya (1945)
Keep it simple!

Excursions in Number Theory, Ogilvy (1988)
Lots of cool mathematical toys, some of which may appear on this website at an indeterminate future point.

computing

Programming in Haskell, Hutton (2007)
When mathematicians study functions, they place a lot of importance on characterizing their inputs and outputs (in particular, the spaces between which the functions act). When I was learning to program, I remember getting tripped up by not thinking in this way. Then I remember thinking that this kind of conceptual rigor would make programming much easier. Then I learned that Haskell is based on just these kinds of ideas.

etc

On the Nature of Things, Lucretius (1st century BC)
Lucretius more or less predicts the fact that matter is composed of atoms, which is amazing. In addition, his description of the theory (as well as its encompassing worldview) takes the shape of an incredibly vivid and dramatic poem. One of my favorite books ever. Run, don't walk.

A History of Astronomy, Pannekoek (1961)
The original data scientists.

Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future, Thiel (2014)
