reading list

This list reflects some of the (mostly science-related) things I've been reading lately.

biology

An Introduction to Systems Biology: Design Principles of Biological Circuits, Alon (2006)

Introduction to Computational Genomics: A Case Studies Approach, Cristianini & Hahn (2006)

The Ideas of Biology, Bonner, JT (1962):s
This is a well-written discussion of some of the fundamental concepts in biology. Bonner outlines a rapidly-changing field at a stage in its development where big contemporary trends were still beyond the horizon, but scientists were beginning to anticipate their existence. An interesting illustration of biological principles, and also of the way that science takes shape.

The Double Helix: A Personal Account of the Discovery of the Structure of DNA, Watson (1968)

Molecular Biology for Computer Scientists, Hunter (1993)

mathematics

How to Solve It: A New Aspect of Mathematical Method, Polya (1945)
Keep it simple!

Excursions in Number Theory, Ogilvy (1988)

computing

Programming in Haskell, Hutton (2007)
When mathematicians study functions, they place a lot of importance on carefully characterizing the function's inputs and outputs (in particular, the spaces between which the function acts). When I was learning to program, I remember getting tripped up by not thinking in this way. Then I remember thinking that this kind of conceptual structure would make programming so much easier. Then I realized that Haskell is built on just this kind of thinking.

etc

Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future, Thiel (2014)

The Man Who Mistook His Wife for a Hat, Sacks (1985)
How all popular science books should be written.

A History of Astronomy, Pannekoek (1961)
The original data scientists.
